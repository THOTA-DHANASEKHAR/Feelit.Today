Fact Tables:

1. Definition:

  • A fact table is a central table in a data warehouse schema that contains quantitative data (facts) about a business process or event. It typically includes numerical measures and foreign keys that link to dimension tables.

2. Key Characteristics:

Measures: Numeric values or metrics that represent the performance or characteristics of the business process, such as sales revenue, quantity sold, or profit.

Foreign Keys: Links to dimension tables, connecting the fact table to descriptive attributes stored in those dimensions.

Granularity: Defines the level of detail at which facts are recorded. Granularity can vary based on business requirements.

3. Components of a Fact Table:

Primary Key: A primary key that uniquely identifies each record in the fact table.

Foreign Keys: Links to the primary keys in dimension tables, forming relationships between the fact table and dimension tables.

Measures: Numeric values representing the quantitative aspects of the business process.

Date/Time Stamps: Timestamps indicating when the business events occurred.

4. Types of Measures:

Additive Measures: Measures that can be aggregated across all dimensions. For example, sales revenue is additive because it can be summed across different products, customers, and time periods.

Semi-Additive Measures: Measures that can be aggregated across some dimensions but not all. An example is a bank account balance, which can be summed across customers but not across time.

Non-Additive Measures: Measures that cannot be aggregated. Ratios or percentages are examples of non-additive measures.

5. Grain of the Fact Table:

  • The grain of a fact table defines the level of detail at which facts are recorded. For example, a sales fact table may have a daily, weekly, or monthly grain, depending on business requirements.

6. Fact Table Design:

  • Fact tables are designed to support efficient querying and reporting. They are often denormalized for performance reasons, containing redundant data to minimize the need for joins during analysis.

7. Dimensions in Fact Tables:

  • Fact tables have foreign keys that link to dimension tables. These dimensions provide context and descriptive attributes to the quantitative measures in the fact table.

8. Real-world Example:

  • In a retail data warehouse, a fact table for sales transactions might include measures like sales amount, quantity sold, and discount. It would have foreign keys linking to dimension tables for products, customers, time, and locations.

9. Fact Table Types:

Transaction Fact Table: Records individual business transactions at the most detailed level.

Snapshot Fact Table: Captures a snapshot of business metrics at a specific point in time.

Cumulative Fact Table: Aggregates values over a period, such as cumulative sales for a month.

10. Aggregate Fact Tables:

  • Aggregate fact tables store pre-aggregated values to improve query performance for summary or aggregated queries. They are often used for large datasets where querying the detailed fact table might be computationally expensive.

11. Usage in Business Intelligence:

  • Fact tables are fundamental to business intelligence and data analysis. They serve as the primary source for creating reports, dashboards, and analytics that provide insights into business performance.

12. Fact Table Modeling Considerations:

  • Considerations include choosing the appropriate grain, selecting the right measures, and optimizing the structure for query performance. Design decisions should align with business requirements and analytical needs.

Understanding the role and design principles of fact tables is crucial for building effective data warehouses that support meaningful analysis and reporting in business intelligence environments.

Snowflake Schema:

1. Definition:

  • The snowflake schema is a type of database schema used in data warehousing where a central fact table is connected to multiple dimension tables. However, unlike the star schema, the dimension tables in a snowflake schema are normalized, meaning they are further divided into sub-dimensions.

2. Structure:

  • The fact table sits at the center, surrounded by normalized dimension tables. Each dimension table is often divided into sub-dimensions, forming a structure that resembles the shape of a snowflake.

3. Components:

Fact Table: The central table containing quantitative and numeric data (measures) related to business processes or events.

Dimension Tables: These tables store descriptive attributes or dimensions related to the business process. Unlike the star schema, dimension tables in a snowflake schema may have multiple levels of normalization, leading to sub-dimensions.

4. Normalization in Snowflake Schema:

  • Dimension tables in a snowflake schema are normalized to reduce redundancy and improve data integrity. This involves breaking down the dimension tables into sub-dimensions, creating a more structured and normalized database.

5. Advantages:

Normalizing Data: Reduces redundancy in dimension tables, saving storage space.

Improved Data Integrity: Ensures that data is consistent and accurate.

Ease of Maintenance: Easier to update and maintain due to the normalized structure.

6. Disadvantages:

Complexity: More complex to understand and query compared to a star schema.

Query Performance: May result in slower query performance due to multiple joins.

7. Example:

  • In a retail data warehouse, a snowflake schema might have a fact table recording sales transactions, and dimension tables for products, customers, time, and locations. The dimension tables, such as the product dimension, may be further divided into sub-dimensions like product category and supplier.

8. Querying:

  • Queries in a snowflake schema involve more joins compared to a star schema. Users need to navigate through multiple levels of normalization, which can make queries more complex and potentially slower.

9. Hierarchy in Snowflake Schema:

  • Dimension tables in a snowflake schema often have hierarchical structures. For example, a time dimension may have hierarchies like year, quarter, month, and day, and each of these levels could be a separate table.

10. Real-world Example:

  • In a human resources data warehouse, a snowflake schema might have a fact table recording employee performance metrics, and dimension tables for employees, departments, and time. The employee dimension may be normalized into sub-dimensions for personal details and job history.

11. Use Cases:

  • Snowflake schemas are often used in scenarios where data integrity is a top priority and storage space efficiency is crucial. They may be preferred in environments where data updates and maintenance are frequent.

12. Scalability:

  • Like star schemas, snowflake schemas are scalable and can handle large volumes of data. The normalization, however, may introduce additional complexity.

Understanding the snowflake schema is important for data warehouse designers and administrators, especially when making decisions about schema design based on the specific requirements and trade-offs of a given project.
